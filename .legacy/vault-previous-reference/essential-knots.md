# Essential Knots for Survival

**Category**: Tools & Skills
**Difficulty**: Beginner to Intermediate
**Time Required**: Practice until automatic
**Materials**: Rope, cordage, or improvised line
**Season**: All seasons

## Overview

Knot-tying is a fundamental survival skill. The right knot can secure shelter, haul gear, create tools, and save lives. Master these essential knots and understand when to use each.

## Knot Terminology

**Standing End**: The long, inactive end of rope
**Working End**: The end you're tying with
**Bight**: A U-shaped bend in rope (not crossed)
**Loop**: Rope crossed over itself forming a circle
**Turn**: One wrap around an object

## The Essential Eight (Master These First)

### 1. Bowline - "King of Knots"

**Purpose**: Creates fixed loop that won't slip or bind

**Uses**:
- Rescue (loop around person)
- Securing to tree/post
- End of climbing rope
- Makeshift harness

**How to Tie** ("Rabbit Hole" Method):
1. Make small loop (rabbit hole) in standing end
2. Pass working end up through loop (rabbit out of hole)
3. Around behind standing end (around tree)
4. Back down through original loop (back in hole)
5. Tighten by pulling standing end and loop

**Mnemonic**: "Rabbit comes out of hole, around the tree, back in the hole"

**Pros**:
- Easy to untie after loading
- Won't slip
- Won't bind tight
- Strong

**Cons**:
- Can loosen if not under load
- Not ideal for slippery rope

**Test**: Should be able to tie one-handed, eyes closed

---

### 2. Square Knot (Reef Knot)

**Purpose**: Join two ropes of equal diameter

**Uses**:
- Tying bandages
- Bundling items
- Temporary rope joining (NOT for critical loads)

**How to Tie**:
1. Right over left and under
2. Left over right and under
3. "Right over left, left over right"

**Visual Check**: Ends should exit parallel to their standing lines

**CRITICAL**:
- **NOT for climbing or life-safety**
- Can slip under certain loads
- Only for equal-diameter ropes
- Can capsize (invert and fail)

**Pros**:
- Quick to tie
- Lays flat
- Easy to untie

**Cons**:
- Can fail under load
- Slips with different diameter ropes

---

### 3. Two Half Hitches

**Purpose**: Attach rope to post, tree, or object

**Uses**:
- Securing tent lines
- Tying boat to dock
- Hanging items
- Quick attachment

**How to Tie**:
1. Pass rope around object
2. Bring working end over standing end and through (half hitch)
3. Repeat second half hitch
4. Tighten against object

**Pros**:
- Very quick
- Easy to adjust
- Reliable
- Easy to untie

**Cons**:
- Can slip on smooth objects
- Needs load to stay tight

---

### 4. Taut-Line Hitch (Adjustable)

**Purpose**: Create adjustable loop that holds under tension

**Uses**:
- Tent guy lines
- Adjustable tarp lines
- Any line needing tension adjustment
- Clothesline

**How to Tie**:
1. Pass rope around anchor (stake, tree)
2. Working end wraps INSIDE loop around standing line (2-3 wraps toward load)
3. One wrap OUTSIDE loop (away from load)
4. Pull tight

**Function**:
- Slides when not under load
- Grips when pulled
- Adjust by sliding knot along rope

**Pros**:
- Adjustable tension
- Holds well
- No hardware needed

**Cons**:
- Can slip on very slick rope
- Takes practice

---

### 5. Clove Hitch

**Purpose**: Quick temporary attachment to pole or post

**Uses**:
- Starting lashings
- Temporary tie-up
- Attaching items to ridge line
- Quick shelter setup

**How to Tie** (Around Object):
1. Make one full turn around object
2. Cross over first turn
3. Pass under second turn
4. Tighten

**How to Tie** (Over End of Pole):
1. Make two loops in rope
2. Place second loop behind first
3. Slip both over end of pole
4. Tighten

**Pros**:
- Very fast
- Works on poles
- Good starting point for lashings

**Cons**:
- Slips if not loaded from correct direction
- Not secure without load
- Can jam tight

---

### 6. Trucker's Hitch (Mechanical Advantage)

**Purpose**: Create 3:1 mechanical advantage for tensioning

**Uses**:
- Securing loads
- Tight ridge lines
- Hauling
- Any time you need serious tension

**How to Tie**:
1. Secure one end to anchor
2. At midpoint, create slip loop (or fixed loop)
3. Pass working end through far anchor
4. Thread working end through the loop (creates pulley)
5. Pull for 3:1 advantage
6. Secure with two half hitches

**Function**: Loop acts as pulley, multiplying pulling force

**Pros**:
- Huge mechanical advantage
- Extremely tight lines possible
- Release and re-adjust easily

**Cons**:
- More complex
- Takes practice
- Can abrade rope at loop

---

### 7. Figure-Eight Loop

**Purpose**: Fixed loop (stronger than bowline)

**Uses**:
- Climbing (end of rope)
- Rescue
- Any critical load
- Alternative to bowline

**How to Tie**:
1. Make bight in rope
2. Make figure-eight with the bight
3. Follow original path back through (rewoven eight)
4. Tighten

**Or** (simpler on-a-bight method):
1. Make bight in rope
2. Treat bight as single line
3. Tie overhand knot, but add extra turn (makes figure-8)
4. Tighten

**Pros**:
- Very strong
- Won't slip
- Easy to inspect
- Climbing standard

**Cons**:
- Can jam tight after heavy load
- Bulkier than bowline

---

### 8. Sheet Bend (Join Different Diameter Ropes)

**Purpose**: Join two ropes of different diameters

**Uses**:
- Extending rope with different size
- Attaching cord to tarp grommet
- Making nets
- Joining improvised cordage

**How to Tie**:
1. Make bight in THICKER rope
2. Pass THINNER rope up through bight
3. Around behind both legs of bight
4. Tuck under itself (between bight and itself)
5. Tighten

**Double Sheet Bend** (more secure):
- Make second wrap around bight before tucking

**Pros**:
- Works with different diameters
- Secure
- Relatively easy to untie

**Cons**:
- Can slip if not dressed properly
- Not for critical life-safety

---

## Intermediate Knots (Next Level)

### Prusik Hitch (Friction Hitch)

**Purpose**: Grips rope when weighted, slides when not

**Uses**:
- Ascending rope
- Safety backup
- Hauling systems
- Clothesline tensioner

**How to Tie**:
1. Use small loop of cord (thinner than main rope)
2. Wrap loop around main rope 3-4 times
3. Thread through itself each time
4. Tighten wraps evenly

**Function**: Grips when loaded, slides when pushed

---

### Timber Hitch

**Purpose**: Attach rope to log or pole for dragging

**Uses**:
- Dragging logs
- Temporary attachment to round objects
- Quick release needed

**How to Tie**:
1. Pass rope around log
2. Wrap working end around standing end
3. Make 3-4 wraps around itself (INSIDE loop)
4. Tighten - grips when pulled

**Releases**: Instantly when load removed

---

### Rolling Hitch

**Purpose**: Lengthwise pull on rope or pole

**Uses**:
- Hauling rope parallel to another
- Slide-and-grip on pole
- Boat fender lines

**How to Tie**:
1. Two wraps around object (toward load direction)
2. Third wrap other direction
3. Tuck under final wrap
4. Tighten

---

## Lashings (Joining Poles)

### Square Lashing (Perpendicular Poles)

**Uses**:
- Shelter frames
- Raft building
- Furniture construction

**Steps**:
1. Clove hitch around one pole
2. Weave over-under pattern (3-4 wraps)
3. Frapping turns (between poles, tightens)
4. Clove hitch on second pole

---

### Diagonal Lashing (Crossing Poles)

**Uses**:
- Bracing
- X-frames
- Shear strength

**Steps**:
1. Timber hitch around crossing
2. Wraps in both V's created by cross
3. Frapping between poles
4. Clove hitch finish

---

### Tripod Lashing

**Uses**:
- Tripod legs
- Cooking tripod
- Shelter pole bundles

**Steps**:
1. Lay three poles parallel
2. Clove hitch on end pole
3. Loose wraps around all three (7-8 wraps)
4. Frapping between each pole
5. Clove hitch on opposite end pole
6. Spread legs into tripod

## Knot Selection Guide

**Situation** → **Best Knot**

- Fixed loop in end of rope → Bowline or Figure-8 Loop
- Join two equal ropes → Square Knot (low-load) or Double Fisherman's
- Join different diameter ropes → Sheet Bend
- Attach to post/tree → Two Half Hitches or Clove Hitch
- Adjustable guy line → Taut-Line Hitch
- Maximum tension needed → Trucker's Hitch
- Drag a log → Timber Hitch
- Climb or rescue → Figure-8 Loop or Bowline
- Quick release → Slip knot variations
- Grip main rope → Prusik Hitch

## Practice Progression

### Stage 1: Basic Competency
- Tie each essential knot correctly
- Understand purpose
- Slow, checking each step

### Stage 2: Speed
- Muscle memory development
- Tie without thinking
- Time yourself

### Stage 3: Difficult Conditions
- One-handed (bowline critical)
- In the dark
- With gloves
- Wet rope
- While fatigued

### Stage 4: Application
- Real-world scenarios
- Choose right knot for situation
- Combine knots (e.g., trucker's hitch uses half hitches)

## Common Mistakes

**Not Dressing the Knot**:
- "Dressing" = arranging strands neatly
- Undressed knots weaker and more likely to fail
- Always tighten and neaten

**Wrong Knot for Application**:
- Square knot for climbing (NEVER)
- Granny knot instead of square (won't hold)
- Using complex knot when simple works better

**Not Testing**:
- Always test knot before trusting with load
- Pull on both ends
- Verify it tightens correctly

**Leaving Too Little Tail**:
- Minimum 6" tail on working end
- Prevents knot from "working loose"
- Safety factor

## Rope Care

**Inspection**:
- Check for fraying
- Sun damage (UV degrades nylon)
- Chemical exposure
- Wear at high-stress points

**Storage**:
- Coil properly (prevents kinks)
- Dry before storing
- Out of direct sun
- Away from chemicals/solvents

**Field Repairs**:
- Whip frayed ends (wrap tightly with small cord)
- Cut out damaged sections
- Fuse synthetic rope ends (carefully melt)

## Improvised Cordage

**Natural Materials**:
- Inner bark (basswood, cedar, willow)
- Plant fibers (yucca, cattail leaves, nettle)
- Sinew (animal tendons)
- Roots (spruce, willow)
- Vines (grape, wisteria)

**Improvised**:
- Paracord inner strands (7 strands in 550 cord)
- Clothing strips (fabric)
- Plastic bags (twisted or braided)
- Wire (electrical, baling)

## Knot Strength

**General Rules**:
- Any knot weakens rope (15-50% strength loss)
- Bends (sharp curves) create stress points
- More wraps generally = stronger but bulkier
- Wet rope is weaker than dry

**Relative Strength** (% of rope strength):
- Figure-8 Loop: ~75-80%
- Bowline: ~60-75%
- Square Knot: ~45%
- Sheet Bend: ~55%

## Safety Notes

**For Life-Safety**:
- Use figure-8 or bowline (not square knot)
- Inspect knots regularly
- Understand load ratings of rope
- When in doubt, use stronger/more secure knot

**Redundancy**:
- Backup critical knots
- Multiple anchors
- Safety lines
