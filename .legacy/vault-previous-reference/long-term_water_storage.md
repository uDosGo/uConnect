# Long-Term Water Storage

**Category:** Water
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Generated Date:** 2024-07-29

### Overview

Water is the most critical element for human survival, often cited as being survivable for only three days without it. In any emergency scenario—be it a natural disaster, infrastructure failure, or widespread societal disruption—access to safe, potable water can quickly become compromised. Municipal water systems can fail, pipes can break, and tap water can become contaminated, leaving individuals and families without a life-sustaining supply. Therefore, establishing a robust long-term water storage plan is an indispensable component of comprehensive survival preparedness.

Long-term water storage goes beyond simply having a few bottles in a bug-out bag; it involves preparing and storing sufficient quantities of water for extended periods, often weeks to months, at a primary residence or retreat location. The goal is to create a self-sufficient water reserve that can sustain you and your dependents when external water sources are unavailable or unsafe. This guide will provide practical steps to ensure your stored water remains potable and accessible when you need it most.

It is crucial to understand that while proper storage can keep water safe for very long durations, regular inspection and occasional rotation are still best practices. This ensures container integrity and maintains peace of mind. By carefully planning, selecting appropriate containers, properly treating and storing water, and implementing a maintenance schedule, you can create a reliable lifeline that will prove invaluable during an emergency.

### Materials Needed

*   **Food-Grade Water Containers:** New, clean containers explicitly labeled "food-grade" or for "drinking water."
    *   Examples: 5-gallon rigid plastic jugs (HDPE #2), 55-gallon plastic drums, clear PET #1 bottles (only if stored in total darkness), stainless steel containers.
    *   Avoid: Containers that previously held non-food liquids (e.g., chemicals, gasoline), non-food-grade plastics, fragile glass (unless carefully managed).
*   **Unscented Household Bleach (5.25%-6% Sodium Hypochlorite):** For purification and sanitization. Ensure it does *not* contain dyes, scents, or other additives. Check expiration date for potency.
*   **Water Purification Tablets (Optional Backup):** Iodine, chlorine dioxide, or other tablets for emergency purification of questionable stored water.
*   **Water Filter (Optional):** A high-quality gravity, pump, or bottle filter for pre-filtration of non-municipal water or for post-storage purification if water quality is suspect.
*   **Siphon Pump or Spigot:** Especially for large containers (e.g., 55-gallon drums) for easy dispensing.
*   **Sanitized Funnel:** For filling containers cleanly.
*   **Clean Gloves:** To maintain hygiene during handling.
*   **Permanent Marker:** For labeling containers with fill dates and treatment methods.
*   **Logbook/Documentation:** For tracking inventory, fill dates, and rotation schedule.

### Step-by-Step Instructions

#### 1. Assess Your Water Needs

Determine the minimum amount of water your household requires.
1.  **Basic Calculation:** The generally accepted minimum is 1 gallon (approximately 3.8 liters) of water per person per day for drinking and minimal hygiene.
2.  **Expanded Calculation:** For more comfortable living, including cooking and basic personal hygiene, aim for 2-4 gallons per person per day.
3.  **Factor in Others:** Include all household members, pets (dogs, cats: 0.5-1 gallon/day depending on size), and potential guests.
4.  **Determine Duration:** Decide how long you want your stored supply to last (e.g., 2 weeks, 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, 1 year).
    *   *Example:* For a family of four, aiming for 2 gallons/person/day for 3 months: 4 people * 2 gallons/person/day * 90 days = 720 gallons.

#### 2. Select and Acquire Appropriate Containers

The right container is paramount for safe long-term storage.
1.  **Material:** Opt for food-grade plastic (HDPE #2 or PET #1 are common and safe), stainless steel, or specific water bricks. Avoid non-food-grade plastics, old milk jugs (which degrade and leach), or containers that previously held anything other than food or water.
2.  **Size:** Balance ease of handling with storage efficiency. 5-gallon jugs are manageable, while 55-gallon drums offer bulk storage but require a siphon or spigot.
3.  **Opacity:** Opaque or dark-colored containers are preferred as they block light, which inhibits algae growth. If using clear containers, they *must* be stored in absolute darkness.
4.  **Durability:** Choose robust containers that can withstand minor impacts and the weight of water without cracking or leaking over time.

#### 3. Prepare Your Water Source

The quality of your source water affects storage duration and required treatment.
1.  **Municipal Tap Water:** This is often the easiest and safest to store, as it is usually already treated with chlorine.
2.  **Well Water:** Generally good, but may require pre-filtration and additional purification if you are unsure of its purity or local well conditions.
3.  **Rainwater/Natural Sources (Streams, Lakes):** These require the most rigorous pre-treatment, including filtration to remove sediment and boiling or chemical purification to kill pathogens, *before* storage.

#### 4. Clean and Sanitize Containers

Proper cleaning prevents contamination of your stored water.
1.  **Initial Wash:** Wash new or reused containers thoroughly with hot water and dish soap. Rinse multiple times to remove all soap residue.
2.  **Sanitization Solution:** Prepare a sanitizing solution using unscented household bleach (5.25%-6% sodium hypochlorite). Mix 1 teaspoon of bleach per gallon of water.
3.  **Sanitize:** Pour the sanitizing solution into each container, cap tightly, and shake vigorously to ensure all interior surfaces are coated. Let stand for at least 30 seconds for new containers, or 2 minutes for reused containers.
4.  **Drain & Air Dry:** Pour out the sanitizing solution. Do NOT rinse with untreated tap water, as this can reintroduce contaminants. Allow the containers to air dry completely upside down or proceed directly to filling.

#### 5. Purify Water for Storage

This step is critical for preventing bacterial growth and ensuring long-term potability.
1.  **For Municipal Tap Water (most common):**
    *   If your tap water is already chlorinated, you may only need to add a small amount of extra bleach to ensure long-term stability. Add 2 drops of unscented household bleach (5.25%-6%) per liter (or quart) of water, or 8 drops per gallon.
    *   Stir or gently shake the container to mix.
    *   Let stand for at least 30 minutes before sealing. A slight chlorine odor is normal.
2.  **For Non-Municipal or Untreated Water (well, rainwater, natural sources):**
    *   **Pre-filtration:** First, filter the water through a clean cloth, coffee filter, or dedicated sediment filter to remove large particles.
    *   **Boiling (preferred):** Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes at altitudes above 6,500 feet / 2,000 meters). Allow to cool completely before proceeding.
    *   **Chemical Treatment (alternative):** If boiling isn't possible, use the bleach method as described for municipal water, but double the dosage: 4 drops per liter/quart OR 16 drops per gallon. Mix well and let stand for at least 30 minutes (preferably several hours) before sealing.

#### 6. Fill and Seal Containers

Minimizing air space is crucial for long-term storage.
1.  **Use a Clean Funnel:** Carefully fill each sanitized container using a clean, sanitized funnel.
2.  **Fill Completely:** Fill the containers almost completely to the brim, leaving as little air space as possible. This reduces the amount of oxygen available for bacterial or algal growth.
3.  **Cap Tightly:** Securely fasten the caps or lids. Ensure a watertight seal to prevent contamination and evaporation.

#### 7. Label and Document

Maintain a clear record for effective rotation.
1.  **Label Each Container:** Use a permanent marker to clearly label each container with the date it was filled and the purification method used (e.g., "Tap Water + Bleach, 2024-07-29").
2.  **Maintain a Logbook:** Keep a separate inventory log that includes the number of containers, their sizes, fill dates, and a planned rotation schedule. This helps you track your supply and ensures older water is used first.

#### 8. Select a Storage Location

Optimal storage conditions preserve water quality.
1.  **Cool Temperature:** Store water in a cool place, ideally between 50-70°F (10-21°C). Heat accelerates chemical reactions and plastic degradation.
2.  **Darkness:** Essential to prevent algae growth and reduce plastic degradation. Basements, cool pantries, or dedicated closets are ideal. If storing clear containers, they *must* be completely shielded from light.
3.  **Dry Environment:** Prevents mold and mildew on containers and surrounding areas.
4.  **Off the Floor (Optional):** Storing containers on pallets or elevated surfaces can protect them from flooding or moisture and improve air circulation.
5.  **Away from Chemicals:** Crucially, never store water containers near gasoline, pesticides, paints, solvents, or other chemicals. Plastics are semi-permeable, and fumes can leach into the water over time, making it toxic.
6.  **Accessible but Secure:** Choose a location that is easily accessible in an emergency but safe from accidental damage, tampering, or theft.

### Maintenance and Rotation

Even properly stored water benefits from occasional checks.
1.  **Regular Inspection:** Every 6-12 months, inspect all containers for leaks, cracks, bulging, or any signs of damage. Check water clarity through the container walls if possible.
2.  **Rotation Schedule:**
    *   Water purified with bleach from a municipal source and stored correctly can remain safe indefinitely according to many experts, but rotating every 5 years is a conservative and recommended practice.
    *   Water from untreated sources (wells, rainwater) or those with minimal purification should be rotated more frequently, perhaps every 1-2 years.
    *   Develop a "first in, first out" system. Use older stored water for daily tasks (watering plants, cleaning, bathing) and replenish with fresh, newly treated water.

### Safety Considerations

*   **Bleach Safety:** Handle bleach carefully. Do not mix with other cleaning agents (especially ammonia). Always use unscented, regular household bleach (5.25%-6% sodium hypochlorite). Keep out of reach of children.
*   **Container Integrity:** Immediately discard any container that shows signs of bulging, cracking, or leakage.
*   **Chemical Contamination:** Reiterate that storing water near hazardous chemicals can render it undrinkable.
*   **Taste/Smell Check:** Before consuming stored water, always perform a visual and olfactory check. If the water looks cloudy, has an unusual color, or smells foul, do not consume it. Filter and re-purify, or discard if you suspect chemical contamination.
*   **Boil if in Doubt:** If you have any doubts about the safety of your stored water, boil it for 1 minute before consumption.
*   **Avoid Unknown Plastics:** Only use containers specifically designed and labeled for food/water storage.

### Common Mistakes

*   **Using Non-Food-Grade Containers:** A primary mistake that can lead to chemical leaching and toxic water.
*   **Using Scented or "Splashless" Bleach:** These contain additives unsafe for consumption. Only use regular, unscented bleach.
*   **Inadequate Container Cleaning/Sanitization:** Introduces bacteria and mold, compromising water quality.
*   **Not Purifying Source Water:** Failing to treat non-municipal water before storage guarantees contamination.
*   **Leaving Excessive Air Space:** Promotes bacterial and algal growth due to oxygen availability.
*   **Storing in Direct Sunlight or Warm Areas:** Accelerates degradation of both water quality and plastic containers.
*   **Storing Near Chemicals:** Fumes permeate plastic, contaminating the water.
*   **Infrequent Rotation or Inspection:** Stored water can degrade or containers can fail without your knowledge.
*   **Underestimating Water Needs:** Not storing enough water for the household's actual requirements during an extended emergency.
*   **Lack of Backup Purification:** Relying solely on stored water without a means to purify it again should it become questionable.
