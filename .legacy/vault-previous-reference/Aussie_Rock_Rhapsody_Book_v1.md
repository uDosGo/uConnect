AUSSIE ROCK RHAPSODY
Book & Scene Breakdown — Full Draft
Based on the Overview & Score v1.1

---

> A note on style: Dialogue is natural. Characters don't explain themselves. Humour lands in the silence after the line. Songs carry the emotional weight — scenes set it up. The Austen Tayshus puns are punchlines, not wallpaper. The band plays live on stage. The backdrop scrolls.

---

ACT ONE

---

SCENE 1: THE FARM
*Sounds of Then – This Is Australia* (Ganga Gang)

The backdrop settles on a wide, sun-bleached farm. Red dirt. A rusted water tank. A Hills Hoist with one good arm. Morning.

DAD is already at it — fixing a fence post with the focused efficiency of a man who hasn't stopped moving in thirty years. His movements are unhurried but certain. He knows this land.

JIMMY emerges from the house, boots unlaced, squinting at the sun like it's personally offended him. He's beautiful in that accidental way. He carries two mugs of tea. Hands one to DAD without a word.

They stand together looking out at the property. A long beat.

DAD: (doesn't look at him) Gate's still open.

JIMMY: I'll get it.

(He doesn't move. Neither does Dad. They drink their tea.)

DAD: Merinos got out again last night.

JIMMY: How many?

DAD: Enough.

(Another long beat.)

JIMMY: I had that dream again.

DAD: Mm.

JIMMY: The one where I'm—

DAD: Yep.

(Dad drains his mug and heads back to the fence. Jimmy watches him go, then looks back at the horizon. Something out there. He can't name it yet.)

The band lifts. The music rises from the land itself — ancient, wide, aching. BELLA and SISTER GIRL appear at the edge of the property, walking the fence line. They don't see Jimmy. They're in their own world, speaking in low voices, laughing about something.

Jimmy notices them. Watches. Bella glances back once, briefly, then they're gone.

The song swells. This is Australia. It just is.

---

SCENE 2: FATHER AND SON
*Flame Trees* (Cold Chisel) **vs** *Working Class Man* (Jimmy Barnes)

Later that day. DAD is under the ute. JIMMY is meant to be helping. He's sitting on the wheel arch, not helping.

JIMMY: Mick Garret's brother got a job in Brisbane.

DAD: (from under the ute) Good for Mick Garret's brother.

JIMMY: Said it pays alright.

DAD: Hand us the half-inch.

(Jimmy passes the wrong spanner. Dad looks at it. Puts it down. Reaches for the right one himself.)

JIMMY: I'm just saying.

DAD: I heard you.

JIMMY: There's stuff out there, Dad. You know? Stuff happening.

DAD: (beat) Plenty happening here.

JIMMY: That's not—... yeah.

(Silence. The ute ticks in the heat.)

DAD: (still under the ute) Your grandfather built this place with two good hands and one bad back. Didn't have time to wonder what was happening in Brisbane.

JIMMY: Different times.

DAD: (slides out, looks at him) You reckon?

(He holds Jimmy's gaze for a moment — not unkind, just steady. Then he's back under the ute.)

DAD: Gate's still open by the way.

The band begins. Two guitars finding each other — one searching, one rooted. The songs play against each other: Dad singing his world into the soil, Jimmy singing his restlessness into the sky. They're not fighting. They just want different things. That's almost worse.

The backdrop shifts slowly — the farm in golden late afternoon light, beautiful and enormous and utterly indifferent.

---

SCENE 3: ORIGINAL SIN / ARE YOU OLD ENOUGH
*Original Sin* (INXS) **vs** *Are You Old Enough* (Dragon)

The dam. Blue sky. BELLA sits reading on the bank. SISTER GIRL lies beside her, hat over her face, not reading anything.

SISTER GIRL: He was looking at you again.

BELLA: No he wasn't.

SISTER GIRL: He was absolutely looking at you.

BELLA: He was looking at the dam.

SISTER GIRL: The dam. (beat) You were sitting in front of the dam.

(Bella turns a page.)

SISTER GIRL: He's pretty though. In a helpless sort of way.

BELLA: Mm.

SISTER GIRL: Like a golden retriever that can't find its ball.

BELLA: (almost smiles) Don't.

JIMMY appears on the ridge above, sees them, does a very unconvincing job of pretending he wasn't looking for them.

JIMMY: (calling down) Hey.

BELLA: (not looking up) Hey.

(Jimmy scrambles down the bank in a way that is not elegant. Sister Girl watches with open delight. He arrives, slightly out of breath.)

JIMMY: Good book?

BELLA: Yeah.

JIMMY: What's it about?

BELLA: (beat) Stuff.

(Sister Girl makes a noise that could be a cough.)

JIMMY: Cool. Cool. (beat) I'm Jimmy.

BELLA: I know who you are, Jimmy.

(Sister Girl gives up and just grins at the sky.)

Jimmy sits. Not too close. Close enough. The songs begin to weave — the pull of attraction and the wisdom of caution. Bella knows something Jimmy doesn't yet. She sees him clearly. He sees her and forgets everything else.

But just beyond the edge of the light — EVIE. Watching. Calculating. Already interested.

---

SCENE 4: THE MILKBAR
*The Boys Light Up* (Australian Crawl)

The milkbar. Afternoon sun through dirty windows. JIMMY and DAVO at the counter, two Cokes, not much happening. This is their natural habitat.

DAVO: If you had to. Crocodile or shark.

JIMMY: What's the question?

DAVO: Fight. Which one do you pick.

JIMMY: Where are we?

DAVO: Doesn't matter.

JIMMY: It matters. If we're in the water—

DAVO: You're on a sandbank.

JIMMY: Then the croc.

DAVO: Wrong. Shark every time.

JIMMY: How is that—

DAVO: Eyes. You poke a shark in the eyes, it leaves you alone. Everyone knows that.

JIMMY: Who told you that?

DAVO: (long pause) Someone.

(They drink their Cokes.)

JIMMY: I reckon a croc, mate.

DAVO: You're going to die on that sandbank.

(The door opens. EVIE walks in. The afternoon light seems to rearrange itself around her. Jimmy and Davo both stop mid-sip. A beat of pure biological response.)

EVIE: (to the woman behind the counter) Chiko roll thanks, love.

(She doesn't look at them. She doesn't need to. She knows exactly what's happening.)

DAVO: (very quietly, to Jimmy) Shark. Forget what I said. Shark.

(Jimmy says nothing. He's already gone.)

The band kicks in. Proper. The boys light up — all swagger and sunburn and absolutely zero self-awareness. The backdrop blazes with afternoon colour, the milkbar frozen in amber like every summer afternoon that ever mattered.

---

SCENE 5: INTO THE CITY
*Evie* (Stevie Wright)

Outside the milkbar. EVIE leans against the wall eating her Chiko roll. JIMMY approaches with the confidence of someone who has already rehearsed this moment and none of it is working.

JIMMY: So, uh... you from round here?

EVIE: (eating) No.

JIMMY: Visiting?

EVIE: Passing through.

JIMMY: Right. Right. (beat) Where to?

EVIE: (looks at him properly for the first time) Sydney.

(A beat.)

JIMMY: Sydney's good. I've... I've heard good things.

EVIE: You've never been.

JIMMY: No, but—

EVIE: You want to come?

(Jimmy blinks. That was not what he was expecting.)

JIMMY: I—... when?

EVIE: (finishes her Chiko roll, pushes off the wall) Tomorrow.

(She walks away. Jimmy watches her go.)

DAVO appears beside him from nowhere.

DAVO: What'd she say?

JIMMY: She asked me to go to Sydney.

DAVO: (long pause) What about the croc?

The band begins — Evie's song, all electric heat and danger, the kind of song that makes bad decisions feel inevitable. The backdrop scrolls: the flat country giving way to highway, highway giving way to the first suggestion of city lights on the horizon.

BELLA watches from across the street. She sees everything. She says nothing.

---

SCENE 6: YOU SHOOK ME ALL NIGHT LONG
*You Shook Me All Night Long* (AC/DC) — *Evie sings*

The city. A share house somewhere loud. The backdrop has arrived at Sydney — neon, noise, a chaos of angles after all that open space.

EVIE performs this song — not to the audience, to Jimmy. She owns it completely. She's the one doing the shaking. He is absolutely shook. She sings "he shook me all night long" with a smile that says she knows exactly what she's doing and she's not sorry about any of it.

This is pure spectacle. Let it be.

Jimmy is somewhere between euphoria and mild panic. The city swallows them both.

---

SCENE 7: SPIRAL
*Run to Paradise* (Choirboys)

Weeks later. The city share house, messier now. Beer cans. Takeaway containers. Jimmy and Evie tangled in a life that has no edges.

Back on the farm — BELLA is getting ready. She's heard things. She's not the kind of person who waits to find out.

SISTER GIRL: (watching her pack a bag) Don't.

BELLA: I'm just going to see.

SISTER GIRL: You know what you're going to see.

BELLA: (beat) Maybe.

SISTER GIRL: And then what?

(Bella zips the bag. No answer.)

SISTER GIRL: You don't have to do this.

BELLA: I know.

SISTER GIRL: (quietly) You're better than this.

BELLA: (stops. looks at her) I know that too.

(She picks up the bag and goes. Sister Girl watches the door for a long moment.)

SISTER GIRL: (to the empty room) White people.

The song tears through both worlds simultaneously — Jimmy and Evie burning, Bella on a bus heading toward something she already knows. Running to paradise. Running away from it. Depends which way you're facing.

---

SCENE 8: HOWZAT
*Howzat* (Sherbet)

Outside the city share house. BELLA arrives. Finds the door open. Walks in. Finds what she finds.

She doesn't make a scene. That's not who she is.

JIMMY stumbles out, genuinely pleased to see her, genuinely oblivious. The smile on his face is the worst thing about it.

JIMMY: Bella! What are you doing here? This is — this is amazing—

(He goes to hug her. She steps back. Just slightly. The smile on his face dies by degrees.)

JIMMY: Hey... you alright?

(She looks at him. Really looks at him. She's working out if he's worth being angry at.)

BELLA: (quiet, even) You're a dumb bastard, Jimmy.

(She walks out. He stands there.)

EVIE appears in the doorway behind him, completely unbothered, eating cereal.

EVIE: Friend of yours?

(Jimmy doesn't answer. He watches the door. It doesn't open again.)

The song plays — Howzat. The band makes it sting. The backdrop scrolls past the city without stopping, heading back toward the open country. Back toward the truth of things.

End of Act One — except it isn't over. Not yet.

---

SCENE 9: DUMB THINGS
*Dumb Things* (Paul Kelly)

The farm. The house. DAD's chair. Empty.

JIMMY gets the call. We don't hear the call. We see his face.

He sits on the back step where he and Dad used to drink tea and look at the horizon. He holds his mug. He doesn't drink from it.

DAVO arrives. Sits beside him. Doesn't say anything for a long time.

DAVO: When?

JIMMY: Tuesday.

(Beat.)

DAVO: Did he... was it—

JIMMY: Heart. In the top paddock. Tony Kowalski found him.

(Beat.)

DAVO: (quietly) Ah, mate.

(Silence. The farm around them. All that land.)

JIMMY: I wasn't here.

DAVO: Jimmy—

JIMMY: I was in Sydney eating Evie's cereal and he was dying in the top paddock.

(No answer to that. Davo just stays there. That's enough. That's everything.)

The song carries the weight of all of it — the dumb things we do, the moments we miss, the doors we can't reopen. Paul Kelly wrote it about something else but it fits here perfectly. It always does.

The backdrop holds on the empty paddock. The gate is still open.

---

SCENE 10: ONE WORD
*One Word* (Baby Animals)

Two days later. The city. DAVO has driven six hours. He's standing in the doorway of the share house. JIMMY is exactly where you'd expect him to be.

DAVO: Right. Let's go.

JIMMY: I'm fine.

DAVO: Yeah, you look fantastic. Get your stuff.

JIMMY: Davo—

DAVO: Jimmy. (beat) Get. Your. Stuff.

(Long pause. Jimmy looks around the share house — at Evie's things mixed with his, at the city outside the window, at the life he was trying to have.)

EVIE: (from the other room, not unkind) You should go.

(Jimmy looks at her. Something passes between them — not love, exactly. Something more honest than that.)

EVIE: You were never really here anyway.

(Jimmy picks up his bag. He doesn't argue. He follows Davo out.)

The car trip home. The backdrop scrolls in reverse — city giving way to highway, highway giving way to open country, the land opening up around them like an exhale. Neither of them speaks for a long time.

DAVO: (eventually) Croc or shark.

(Jimmy looks at him.)

DAVO: Just — for the drive.

(The smallest beat. Then:)

JIMMY: Croc.

DAVO: Wrong.

The song carries them home. Baby Animals, raw and certain. One word. Come home.

The farm appears in the backdrop. The gate is still open.

End of Act One.

ACT TWO

---

SCENE 1: WHO CAN IT BE NOW?
*Who Can It Be Now?* (Men at Work)

The farm. A few weeks on. JIMMY has been back long enough to fix the gate.

He stands at BELLA's door. It's a good door. Solid. Not opening.

He knocks again. Waits.

JIMMY: Bella. I know you're in there.

(Nothing.)

JIMMY: I just want to talk.

(Nothing.)

JIMMY: I brought... (looks down at his hands) ...I brought a pie.

(A beat. A long beat. Then SISTER GIRL opens the door. She looks at the pie. She looks at Jimmy. She looks back at the pie.)

SISTER GIRL: What kind.

JIMMY: Beef and mushroom.

(She takes the pie and closes the door.)

(Jimmy stands there. He hears muffled voices inside. Then nothing.)

He sits on the front step and waits. Nothing happens for a very long time. He waits some more.

The song plays from inside — tinny, distant, like it's coming from a clock radio in another room. Who can it be now. The irony is not lost on the audience. Jimmy doesn't get it.

Eventually SISTER GIRL opens the door again.

SISTER GIRL: She says come back when you've got something useful to say.

JIMMY: What does that mean?

SISTER GIRL: She said you'd ask that.

(Door closes. Jimmy sits back down.)

---

SCENE 2: DO YOU SEE WHAT I SEE / WE CAN'T BE BEATEN
*Do You See What I See?* (Hunters & Collectors) **vs** *We Can't Be Beaten* (Rose Tattoo)

The pub. Jimmy and DAVO. Beers. This is a proper conversation — the kind blokes have when they're not looking at each other.

DAVO: So what are you going to say?

JIMMY: I don't know yet.

DAVO: That's the problem.

JIMMY: I know that's the problem. That's why I'm sitting here.

(Beat.)

DAVO: You stuffed it up pretty good.

JIMMY: Thanks, Davo.

DAVO: I'm just saying. Baseline. So you know where you're starting from.

(Jimmy drinks his beer.)

JIMMY: You ever reckon you've looked at something for so long you stopped seeing it?

DAVO: (carefully) Yeah.

JIMMY: Like — it was right there, and you just... went somewhere else instead.

DAVO: (quieter) Yeah.

(A beat that carries more weight than either of them intended. Davo looks at his beer.)

JIMMY: I just want to fix it.

DAVO: Can't fix everything.

JIMMY: No, but—

DAVO: Sometimes you just have to... be different. Like, actually different. Not just say you are.

(Jimmy looks at him. Davo is still looking at his beer. There's something else in there. Jimmy doesn't push it.)

JIMMY: When did you get smart?

DAVO: I've always been smart. You just never noticed.

(Jimmy snorts. Davo grins. The tension breaks.)

JIMMY: Right.

DAVO: I'm very smart. I'm actually the smart one.

JIMMY: You thought you could fight a shark by poking it in the eyes.

DAVO: That is scientifically—

JIMMY: It's not.

DAVO: (long pause) ...Wattle we do about this.

(Jimmy looks at him. That was a pun. A deliberate, terrible pun. In his father's voice.)

JIMMY: (quietly, genuinely moved) Did you just—

DAVO: Don't.

JIMMY: No, that was—

DAVO: I said don't.

(They drink their beers. The songs begin to interweave — searching, then certain. Two blokes working out how to be better. The backdrop glows warm pub light. Outside, the stars are enormous.)

---

SCENE 3: SOLID ROCK
*Solid Rock* (Goanna)

The country. Dawn.

BELLA walks. Not away from anything — toward something. Sister Girl is with her, half a step behind, letting her lead.

This is a different kind of scene. Slower. More space.

SISTER GIRL: (eventually) Where are we going?

BELLA: Just walking.

(They walk.)

SISTER GIRL: You know what Nan would say.

BELLA: Don't.

SISTER GIRL: She'd say you're carrying it in your shoulders.

(Bella says nothing. But her shoulders drop slightly.)

SISTER GIRL: She'd also say that boy has the look of a man who doesn't know what he wants but would die for what he needs.

BELLA: That doesn't even make sense.

SISTER GIRL: Nan said a lot of things that didn't make sense.

(Beat.)

BELLA: She'd also say don't waste your shine on someone who hasn't earned it.

SISTER GIRL: She did say that.

BELLA: All the time.

(They stand on the ridge. The country spreads out beneath them. It's overwhelming in the best way.)

SISTER GIRL: (quietly) You going to be okay?

BELLA: (looking out at the land) I'm always going to be okay.

The song rises — Solid Rock, a prayer and a statement. The backdrop becomes the country itself, deep time, the oldest story. BELLA stands in it. She belongs here in a way that has nothing to do with Jimmy or Evie or any of this. The land is her anchor. It always has been.

Sister Girl watches her sister and quietly thinks about those white people. Not unkindly. Just accurately.

---

SCENE 4: MY GIRL / TAKE ME BACK
*My Girl* (Hoodoo Gurus) **vs** *Take Me Back* (Noiseworks)

The farm. Jimmy working — properly working, the way his father worked. The repetition of it is settling something in him.

DAVO arrives, sits on the fence, watches him.

DAVO: You're not bad at that.

JIMMY: Dad showed me. Years ago.

DAVO: He showed me too. I forgot all of it.

(Jimmy keeps working.)

JIMMY: You remember that time we rode our bikes to the dam and Bella's mob were camped up on the ridge?

DAVO: And you fell in.

JIMMY: I didn't fall in.

DAVO: You absolutely fell in. She laughed. It was the first time I heard her laugh.

(Jimmy stops.)

JIMMY: She's got a great laugh.

DAVO: She's got a great everything, mate. That's your whole problem.

(Jimmy looks at him.)

DAVO: You could see all of it and somehow you still looked away. (beat) How does someone do that?

(No answer. Jimmy goes back to work. The question hangs.)

The songs play against each other — want and regret, the same feeling from two directions. The backdrop drifts through memory: the dam, the bikes, the ridge, the girl on the ridge who saw everything clearly from the start.

---

SCENE 5: THROW YOUR ARMS AROUND ME / BURN FOR YOU
*Throw Your Arms Around Me* (Hunters & Collectors) **vs** *Burn For You* (INXS)

Two separate spaces. BELLA at home. JIMMY at the farm. The backdrop holds them both simultaneously — split, side by side, the same sky over different lives.

They don't know they're singing toward each other. That's the point.

BELLA sings — not to Jimmy, to herself. To the idea of it. To what it could have been.

JIMMY sings — not performing for anyone, just working it out out loud in an empty paddock.

The melodies find each other without the characters ever connecting. The band plays both songs in counterpoint. It's beautiful and it's devastating and nobody says a single word.

Sister Girl wanders through Bella's side of the stage, clocks what's happening, makes a face, goes to make tea.

---

SCENE 6: DREAMWORLD
*Dreamworld* (Midnight Oil)

EVIE is back.

She knocks on BELLA's door. Sister Girl answers.

SISTER GIRL: (looks her up and down) You've got nerve.

EVIE: Is she in?

(Sister Girl considers this. Steps aside.)

BELLA is in the kitchen. She sees Evie. A long moment.

BELLA: What are you doing here?

EVIE: I've been thinking.

BELLA: Good for you.

EVIE: (sits down uninvited) I didn't come to apologise.

BELLA: Wasn't going to accept it anyway.

EVIE: I came because... (she pauses, and this is the most honest she's ever been) I'm tired. I'm so bloody tired of doing the same thing and wondering why nothing changes.

(Bella looks at her. Really looks.)

EVIE: You're the smartest person in a hundred kilometres. I'm not asking for forgiveness. I'm asking if you want to get out of here for a bit.

(Long pause.)

BELLA: (to Sister Girl) Don't say anything.

SISTER GIRL: I wasn't going to say anything.

BELLA: You were absolutely going to say something.

SISTER GIRL: (beat) I was going to say she's right, you are the smartest person in a hundred kilometres. And also this is exactly what Nan was talking about.

BELLA: Nan never met Evie.

SISTER GIRL: Nan knew a Evie in every generation.

(Evie looks between them, slightly lost.)

BELLA: (to Evie) One condition. You don't make me do anything stupid.

EVIE: Where's the fun in that.

BELLA: One condition.

(Beat. Evie grins. Bella almost grins back.)

The song opens up — Midnight Oil, wide and searching. BELLA and EVIE walk out the door together, into their own story. The backdrop shifts and opens. Two women heading somewhere on their own terms. No boys required.

Sister Girl watches them go from the doorway.

SISTER GIRL: (quietly, to no one) White people. (beat) Some of them anyway.

(She goes back inside to finish her tea.)

---

SCENE 7: XANADU
*Xanadu* (Olivia Newton-John)

The pub. Or what has become of the pub.

DAVO is behind a curtain someone has rigged up using a bedsheet and what appears to be fairy lights. Jimmy is sitting at the bar. He has no idea what is about to happen.

JIMMY: Why is there a curtain.

BARMAN: Mate, just drink your beer.

JIMMY: Is there something behind the curtain?

BARMAN: Drink. Your. Beer.

(The music begins. That unmistakable opening. Jimmy sits up straighter.)

(The curtain drops.)

DAVO. In full sequinned glory. He has clearly been waiting his entire life for this moment. He owns every centimetre of this stage.

The pub goes off. Absolute chaos of love and support. People have been waiting for this too — they just didn't know they were waiting.

Jimmy watches his best mate light up the room and on his face is every emotion in sequence — shock, confusion, recognition, and then something that settles into a grin so wide it fixes everything.

After the song, Davo sashays over. Sits beside Jimmy. Tucks a sequin back into place.

DAVO: So.

JIMMY: (still grinning) So.

DAVO: You gonna say something?

JIMMY: (thinks about it) You've always been the smart one.

(Davo laughs. A real laugh. The best kind.)

DAVO: Don't go spreading that around.

The pub sings. The backdrop blazes. This is the scene where nothing is a big deal and that's exactly the point.

---

SCENE 8: RAIN / DANCING IN THE STORM
*Rain* (Dragon) **vs** *Dancing in the Storm* (Boom Crash Opera)

The farm. Night. A real storm building.

JIMMY stands in it. Not sheltering. Just standing in the rain like a man who has decided to stop running from weather.

The storm is full — lightning in the backdrop, rolling across the sky in waves.

This is Jimmy's scene. Nobody else. Just him and the land and everything he's been avoiding.

He thinks about his father. About Bella. About the farm. About Davo behind that curtain in full sequins. About who he actually is versus who he thought he needed to be.

The two songs play against each other — the sorrow of rain, the joy of dancing in it. Jimmy starts in the first song and ends in the second. The shift is not dramatic. It's quiet. Internal. The kind of change that doesn't announce itself.

By the end, he's drenched and smiling. Not because everything is fixed. Because he's decided to be okay with what isn't.

He goes inside. The rain keeps raining. The land soaks it up.

---

SCENE 9: SLICE OF HEAVEN
*Slice of Heaven* (Dave Dobbyn)

Before the finale, a brief and perfect disruption.

A KIWI BLOKE wanders onto the stage from the wrong side. He seems genuinely surprised to be here. He looks around.

KIWI: (to the audience, with genuine warmth) Oh. Oh, you're all here.

(He takes in the entire show — the farm, the drama, the sequins, the crying, all of it.)

KIWI: Bit full on, isn't it. We just... (gestures vaguely) ...have a sausage. Go to the beach. Call it a day.

(Beat.)

KIWI: You're beautiful though. Genuinely. All of you.

(He looks at Jimmy.)

KIWI: Nice farm.

(He looks at where Bella exited.)

KIWI: Smart girl, that one.

(He looks at where Davo made his exit in full sequins.)

KIWI: Legend.

(He nods once at the audience, satisfied.)

KIWI: Anyway. Good on ya.

He strolls back off. The song plays. A Kiwi lovesong to the Tasman. Light, warm, affectionate.

Slice of heaven. Sure. Why not.

FINALE

YOU'RE THE VOICE
*You're The Voice* (John Farnham) — *Full Cast Reprise*

The backdrop opens to the full country — farm, city, coast, bush, sky. Everything at once. Everything together.

The whole cast assembles. Not in a line. Organically — they find each other.

JIMMY and BELLA are near each other. Not together. Near. That's enough. Maybe. We leave that open.

DAVO arrives in full sequins with the poise of a man who has arrived.

SISTER GIRL and EVIE are there — unlikely pair, standing with the quiet satisfaction of women who sorted themselves out.

The KIWI BLOKE is somewhere at the back, slightly confused but supportive.

DAD's chair is on stage. Empty. Present.

The song builds. Farnham at full voltage. The band fully committed. The backdrop blazes with everything Australian and alive and complicated and worth fighting for.

There's no tidy resolution. Bella doesn't fall into Jimmy's arms. Davo doesn't have a big coming out moment — it already happened, in sequins, in a country pub, and everyone cheered. Dad is gone but he's in the land. Evie is neither villain nor redeemed — she's just a person who made choices and is making different ones now.

What there is: everyone on stage, singing, together, not because everything is okay but because they're all still here.

That's the thing. Everyone's still here.

ENCORE

MEDLEY
*10-minute show recap — narration / spoken word / musical highlights*

The band drops into a medley. The NARRATOR — possibly the Kiwi bloke, possibly Davo in a slightly less elaborate outfit, possibly someone entirely new — walks the audience through the whole story again, but faster, funnier, with the benefit of hindsight.

Fragments of songs. Key lines of dialogue delivered rapid-fire.

The Austen Tayshus puns finally let rip — a barrage of them, Dad's greatest hits, delivered by the full cast with the gleeful abandon of people who've been holding them back all night.

Somewhere in the middle, a quiet moment: Jimmy at the gate. He closes it. Finally.

The show ends not with a bang but with a grin.

Full lights. Full sound. Full Australia.

PRODUCTION NOTES

THE DIGITAL BACKDROP

The backdrop is a single continuous landscape that scrolls right to left throughout the show, pausing on each scene location:

Farm — red dirt, eucalypts, corrugated iron, that specific golden light
The Dam — blue shimmer, yellow grass, ridge in the background
The Milkbar — fibro shopfront, hand-painted signs, afternoon light through glass
The Highway — flat, enormous sky, the road going somewhere
The City — Sydney at night, neon and noise, a world made of angles
The Pub — warm and amber, specifically Australian
The Country (Bella's) — deep time, ancient rock, the oldest story
Storm sky — purple and violet, lightning in slow motion
Full Australia — the finale vista, all of it at once

Style: somewhere between Ettamogah Pub illustration and Ginger Meggs comic strip — warm, slightly heightened, unmistakably Australian. Not cartoonish. Affectionate realism.

A NOTE ON THE PUNS

Dad's puns land when nobody's expecting them. They are never explained. They are never repeated. The audience who gets them gets them. The ones who don't still feel the warmth.

Jimmy and Davo's puns are accidental — they don't know they're doing it. That's the whole joke.

The Austen Tayshus finale medley is the one moment they're weaponised deliberately — a celebration of how gloriously stupid and specific Australian English is, delivered with love.
