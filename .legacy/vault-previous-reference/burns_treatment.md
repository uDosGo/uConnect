# Burns Treatment

**Category:** medical
**Difficulty:** beginner
**Generated:** 2025-11-27

This guide provides comprehensive information on treating burns in a wilderness or survival context. While it offers practical advice for managing burns with limited resources, **it is crucial to understand that severe burns are medical emergencies that require professional medical attention as soon as possible.** In a survival situation, your primary goal for significant burns will be to stabilize the patient and facilitate evacuation.

---

## Comprehensive Burns Treatment Guide: Wilderness/Survival Context

### Introduction

Burns are among the most painful and potentially life-threatening injuries in a wilderness setting. They can quickly lead to infection, hypothermia, shock, and long-term disability. Understanding burn classification, immediate treatment, and long-term care, especially with improvised resources, is vital for survival.

### 1. Burn Classification

Understanding the depth of a burn helps determine the severity and required treatment.

*   **1st Degree Burn (Superficial):**
    *   **Appearance:** Red, dry, no blisters.
    *   **Symptoms:** Painful to touch, mild swelling.
    *   **Affected Layers:** Only the outermost layer of skin (epidermis).
    *   **Healing Time:** 3-7 days, usually without scarring.
    *   **Wilderness Context:** Least severe. Focus on pain relief and protection from further irritation.

*   **2nd Degree Burn (Partial Thickness):**
    *   **Appearance:** Red, blistered, moist or weeping surface. Blisters may be small or large.
    *   **Symptoms:** Very painful, significant swelling.
    *   **Affected Layers:** Epidermis and part of the dermis (the layer beneath the epidermis).
    *   **Healing Time:** 2-3 weeks, may leave scarring, especially deeper 2nd-degree burns. High risk of infection.
    *   **Wilderness Context:** Moderate to severe. High priority for infection prevention and pain management. Large 2nd-degree burns (larger than the victim's palm) warrant evacuation.

*   **3rd Degree Burn (Full Thickness):**
    *   **Appearance:** White, leathery, charred, brown, or black. May appear dry and waxy.
    *   **Symptoms:** Often *painless* within the burned area itself (due to nerve damage), but surrounding 2nd-degree areas will be painful. Tissue may feel firm or stiff.
    *   **Affected Layers:** Epidermis, dermis, and potentially underlying fat, muscle, or bone.
    *   **Healing Time:** Requires medical intervention (skin grafting) to heal; extensive scarring.
    *   **Wilderness Context:** **Extremely severe and life-threatening.** High risk of shock, hypothermia, and infection. **Immediate evacuation is paramount.** Focus on shock management, airway/breathing, and preventing further contamination.

### 2. Immediate Treatment Steps (The "Cool, Clean, Cover" Mantra)

These steps are critical in the first few minutes and hours following a burn.

1.  **Stop the Burning Process:**
    *   **Remove Source:** Immediately remove the person from the heat source.
    *   **Extinguish Flames:** If clothes are burning, "stop, drop, and roll" or smother with a blanket, jacket, or dirt.
    *   **Remove Clothing/Jewelry:** Carefully remove any clothing, watches, rings, or other jewelry *from the burned area* that is not stuck to the skin. Clothing can retain heat, and swelling can make jewelry tourniquet-like. **Do NOT pull off clothing that is stuck to the burn.** Cut around it.

2.  **Cool the Burn:**
    *   **Method:** Apply **cool (not ice-cold)** running water over the burn for at least 10-20 minutes. If running water isn't available, immerse the burn in cool water or apply cool, wet compresses, changing them frequently.
    *   **Wilderness Adaptation:** Use water from a clean stream, lake, or your canteen. If only snow is available, pack it around the burn *briefly* (5 minutes maximum) and monitor for frostbite, then switch to a cool damp cloth if possible.
    *   **Purpose:** Cooling reduces pain, swelling, and the depth of the burn.
    *   **Caution:** For large burns (more than 10% of the body surface area, roughly 10 palms), avoid prolonged cooling of the entire body to prevent hypothermia, especially in cold environments. Cool for a few minutes, then cover and focus on warmth.

3.  **Clean the Burn:**
    *   **Method:** Gently wash the burn with clean water and mild soap (if available and clean) to remove dirt, debris, and loose skin. **Do not scrub.**
    *   **Wilderness Adaptation:** Use water that has been boiled and cooled, or the cleanest water source available. If no soap, plain clean water is better than nothing.
    *   **Blisters:** For 2nd-degree burns, do *not* deliberately break blisters. Intact blisters provide a natural sterile barrier against infection. If a blister breaks accidentally, gently clean the area and remove any loose skin, then cover.
    *   **Purpose:** Reduces infection risk.

4.  **Cover the Burn:**
    *   **Method:** Once cooled and cleaned, cover the burn loosely with a clean, dry, non-stick dressing. The dressing should be sterile if possible.
    *   **Wilderness Adaptation (Improvised Dressings):**
        *   **Clean Cloth:** Use the cleanest available fabric â€“ a fresh bandanna, a clean t-shirt (washed and dried if possible), strips from a pillowcase. **Boil the fabric for 5-10 minutes and let it cool before applying for sterilization.**
        *   **Plastic Wrap (Cling Film):** If carried, sterile plastic wrap can be gently applied directly over the burn. It helps reduce pain by protecting nerve endings and prevents fluid loss. Do not wrap tightly.
        *   **Leaves:** In extreme emergencies, some large, clean leaves (e.g., broadleaf plantain, mullein) can be used as a temporary cover *after being thoroughly washed and gently applied*. This is a last resort due to potential contamination.
        *   **Moist Dressing (Debated, Use with Caution):** Some schools of thought suggest a moist dressing for deep burns *initially* to prevent drying, but this increases infection risk and can cause hypothermia. In a wilderness setting, a dry, sterile dressing is generally safer unless you can maintain absolute sterility and warmth.
    *   **Purpose:** Protects the burn from infection, reduces pain, and minimizes fluid loss.

### 3. Pain Management

Pain from burns can be excruciating and lead to shock.

*   **Cooling:** The initial cooling process is the most effective immediate pain reliever.
*   **Over-the-Counter (OTC) Pain Relievers:** If carried, administer ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or acetaminophen (Tylenol) according to package directions.
*   **Elevation:** Elevating the burned extremity (if applicable) can reduce swelling and throbbing pain.
*   **Comfort and Distraction:** Keep the person warm, calm, and distracted. Offer sips of water.
*   **Rest:** Encourage rest and minimize movement of the burned area.

### 4. Infection Prevention

Infection is the leading complication and cause of death in burn patients, especially in a wilderness setting.

*   **Hand Hygiene:** **ALWAYS wash your hands thoroughly** (with soap and clean water, or use hand sanitizer if available) before touching the burn or changing dressings.
*   **Regular Dressing Changes:** Change dressings daily, or more often if they become wet or soiled.
*   **Clean Water:** Use boiled and cooled water for cleaning the burn each time you change dressings.
*   **Antibiotic Ointment (If Available):** For 2nd-degree burns, a thin layer of antibiotic ointment (like bacitracin or triple antibiotic) can be applied *after* cleaning and *before* re-dressing. **Do not use on 3rd-degree burns without medical guidance.**
*   **Monitor for Signs of Infection:**
    *   Increasing redness around the wound
    *   Pus or cloudy discharge
    *   Fever
    *   Increased pain
    *   Foul odor
    *   Red streaks radiating from the burn

### 5. Improvised Dressings and Bandages (Detailing further)

When sterile gauze isn't available, creativity and hygiene are key.

*   **Material Selection:**
    *   **Best:** Cleanest, softest fabric you can find. Cotton is preferred. Think t-shirts, bandannas, handkerchiefs, cloth diapers, pillowcases, tent repair patches.
    *   **Sterilization:** **Boil any fabric for 5-10 minutes, then let it cool completely** before applying. If boiling isn't possible, expose to direct sunlight for several hours (UV rays have some sterilizing effect, but boiling is far superior).
    *   **Avoid:** Lint-producing materials (rough wool), synthetic fabrics that don't breathe well, or anything obviously dirty or irritating.
*   **Application:**
    *   **Layering:** Apply several layers of the improvisational material.
    *   **Loose Fit:** Ensure the dressing is loose enough to allow for swelling and air circulation. Do not wrap tightly.
    *   **Securing:** Use strips of cloth, paracord, duct tape (on the outer dressing, not touching the burn), or even elastic hair ties to secure the dressing.
    *   **Padding:** If possible, add extra padding around the burn to protect it from bumps and pressure.

### 6. Natural Remedies (Use with Extreme Caution)

Natural remedies can offer some relief or protection, but **they are not substitutes for proper medical care and carry risks in an unsterile environment.** Use only for 1st or minor 2nd-degree burns, and always prioritize cleaning.

*   **Aloe Vera:**
    *   **Properties:** Contains compounds that reduce inflammation, pain, and promote healing. It's a natural antiseptic.
    *   **Application:** Break off a leaf from a healthy plant. Squeeze or scrape out the clear gel directly onto a **cleaned** 1st-degree burn or superficial 2nd-degree burn. Repeat several times a day.
    *   **Caution:** Ensure it's true Aloe vera (Aloe barbadensis miller) and not another plant that might be irritating. Wash the leaf thoroughly before use. Do not apply to deep, open wounds.

*   **Honey:**
    *   **Properties:** A natural antiseptic, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and promotes wound healing by drawing out moisture and providing a protective barrier.
    *   **Application:** Apply a thin layer of **raw, unprocessed honey** directly to a **cleaned** 1st or 2nd-degree burn. Cover with a sterile or improvised clean dressing. Change daily.
    *   **Caution:** Use only raw, pure honey. Processed supermarket honey may lack beneficial enzymes. Can be sticky and attract insects.

*   **Plantain (Plantago major/lanceolata):**
    *   **Properties:** Common weed found worldwide. Contains allantoin, which promotes cell growth and is anti-inflammatory and antiseptic.
    *   **Application:** Identify correctly. Wash leaves thoroughly. Chew them briefly or crush them to release juices. Apply the crushed leaves as a poultice directly to minor burns (1st degree or very superficial 2nd degree), securing with a bandage.
    *   **Caution:** Ensure correct identification. Wash thoroughly to remove dirt and pesticides.

### 7. When to Seek Medical Help (Evacuation Priorities)

In a wilderness context, "seeking medical help" means initiating an evacuation or contacting emergency services if possible. **Always err on the side of caution.**

**Initiate evacuation for:**

*   **All 3rd-degree burns, regardless of size.**
*   **2nd-degree burns larger than the size of the victim's palm.**
*   **Burns on critical areas:** Face, hands, feet, genitals, major joints (knees, elbows, shoulders).
*   **Any burn that goes completely around a limb or body part.**
*   **Chemical or electrical burns (see special cases below).**
*   **Suspected inhalation injury:** Hoarse voice, burnt nasal hairs, coughing, shortness of breath, black sputum, confined space fire.
*   **Any burn with signs of infection.**
*   **Burns in children, the elderly, or those with compromised immune systems.**
*   **Circumferential burns:** Burns that completely encircle a limb or digit, as they can cause a tourniquet effect and cut off circulation.

### 8. Long-Term Care and Healing (Post-Acute Phase)

Once immediate threats are managed and, ideally, medical help is en route or has been seen:

*   **Continue Cleaning and Dressing:** Maintain meticulous hygiene.
*   **Moisturize (after skin closes):** Once the burn wound has fully closed (no open areas), moisturize regularly with a natural balm, unscented lotion, or rendered animal fat (if nothing else is available and clean) to keep skin supple and prevent cracking.
*   **Sun Protection:** New skin is very sensitive. Protect healed burn areas from the sun with clothing or sunscreen (once fully healed).
*   **Nutrition and Hydration:** Promote healing with adequate calories, protein, and fluid intake.
*   **Range of Motion:** For burns over joints, gently and regularly move the joint through its full range of motion to prevent contractures (tightening of the skin that restricts movement).
*   **Scar Management:** Massage healed scars gently to improve flexibility and appearance.

### 9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

*   **Applying Ice Directly:** Ice can cause further tissue damage (frostbite) and can lead to hypothermia, especially on large burns. Use cool, not freezing, water.
*   **Applying Butter, Oil, Grease, or Toothpaste:** These trap heat, introduce bacteria, and make cleaning difficult. They offer no therapeutic benefit.
*   **Breaking Blisters (Deliberately):** Blisters are a natural protective barrier. Only drain a large blister that is causing extreme pain or impeding function, using a sterilized needle at its edge, and then keep it covered.
*   **Peeling Off Clothing Stuck to a Burn:** This will cause further damage and pain. Cut around the stuck fabric.
*   **Delaying Cooling:** The sooner a burn is cooled, the less damage it will cause.
*   **Using Dirty Water or Dressings:** This is a direct invitation for infection.
*   **Applying Any Substance Without Knowing What It Is:** Do not experiment with unknown plants or chemicals.

### 10. Special Cases

These require specific and often immediate actions.

*   **Chemical Burns:**
    *   **Action:** **Flush immediately and continuously with large amounts of water** for at least 20-30 minutes, or until emergency medical services arrive/evacuation is complete.
    *   **Remove Contaminated Clothing:** While flushing, carefully remove any clothing or jewelry that has come into contact with the chemical.
    *   **Wilderness Adaptation:** Use the largest available water source (stream, lake) if canteens aren't enough. Ensure water is directed *away* from unburned skin and other people.

*   **Electrical Burns:**
    *   **Action:** **Safety first! Do not touch the victim until the power source is confirmed off or disconnected.** Use a non-conductive object (dry stick, rope) to move the source or victim if absolutely necessary and safe.
    *   **Assessment:** Electrical burns often have a small entry and exit wound, but can cause significant internal damage (heart, nerves, muscles). The extent of external injury does not reflect internal damage.
    *   **Complications:** Risk of cardiac arrest, spinal injury (from being thrown), kidney failure.
    *   **Wilderness Adaptation:** Once safe, check for consciousness, breathing, and pulse. Initiate CPR if necessary. Treat visible burns as 3rd-degree. **Immediate evacuation is mandatory.**

*   **Radiation Burns (e.g., from severe sunburn, nuclear fallout, strong UV sources):**
    *   **Sunburn:** Treat as a 1st or 2nd-degree burn. Cool, moisturize (aloe), protect from further sun exposure.
    *   **Nuclear/Industrial Radiation:** This is an entirely different level of emergency.
        *   **Action:** Distance yourself from the source. Use shielding (earth, concrete).
        *   **Decontamination:** Remove all contaminated clothing (bag it), gently wash exposed skin with soap and water if available. Flush open wounds with clean water.
        *   **Wilderness Context:** Survival from significant radiation exposure involves a completely different set of protocols beyond basic first aid, focusing on shielding, distance, time, and specific medical countermeasures if available. Evacuation to a medical facility is crucial.
