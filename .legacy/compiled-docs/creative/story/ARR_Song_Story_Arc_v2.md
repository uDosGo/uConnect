# **AUSSIE ROCK RHAPSODY**

## **Song-to-Arc Breakdown (v2.0)**

Updated to reflect integrated Book v2.0

Each song is mapped to its emotional beat, scene placement, character arc phase, and key dramatic function.

---
## **ACT ONE**

---
**SONG 1-01: Sounds of Then — This Is Australia** (Gangajang)

This song opens Scene 1 on the farm and introduces the full cast. It establishes the world before any plot mechanics begin. The land, heat, and sky are present as living forces. Bella speaks first, grounding the story immediately. The song runs in full and functions as a miniature of the entire show. Evie drifts through with the sense of someone who already knows how the story ends. Dad never looks up from his bass line. The lyric “Laugh and think, this is Australia” frames the show’s emotional landscape.

---
**SONG 1-02: Working Class Man Intro | Flame Trees | Working Class Man Outro** (Jimmy Barnes | Cold Chisel)

Placed in Scene 2, this father-and-son sequence sets up the fracture between Dad and Jimmy. The structure sandwiches Flame Trees inside Working Class Man to reveal the emotional gap between perception and reality.

Working Class Man represents how Jimmy sees his father: proud, stoic, unbreachable. Flame Trees reveals what Dad is actually carrying—memory, regret, and the quiet beauty and trap of staying. The reprise of Working Class Man returns us to Jimmy’s limited understanding.

The key lyrics underscore this split: “He did his time in Vietnam… He’s a simple man with a heart of gold,” contrasted with “There’s no change, there’s no pace… It just makes it harder to believe she won’t be around.” Dad’s understated pun about strain in the fence wire hints at the emotional strain he cannot name.

---
**SONG 1-03a: Are You Old Enough** (Dragon — NZ)

In Scene 3 at the dam, Niko enters as the outsider mirror. The song poses a question that resonates beyond romance: is the country ready to grow up? Sung by a New Zealander, it carries affectionate clarity. The lyric “Are you old enough for love?” reframes the emotional stakes for both Jimmy and Australia itself. The song’s New Zealand origin is later acknowledged in Act Two.

---
**SONG 1-03b: Original Sin** (INXS)

Still at the dam, this full-number duet between Jimmy and Bella embodies charged possibility. No one touches, yet the tension is enormous. Sister Girl observes, wary but quietly hopeful.

The lyric “Dream on white boy / Dream on black girl / Then wake up to a brand new day” captures the social and personal weight behind their connection. The moment marks first contact—full of potential and danger.

---
**SONG 1-04: The Boys Light Up** (Australian Crawl)

Scene 4 at the milkbar is a comic peak and Evie’s true entrance. Jimmy and Davo are at their most alive—ridiculous and full of themselves. Evie watches like a naturalist observing a species in the wild. Bella watches Evie watching. Sister Girl eats a Chiko Roll and delivers cultural commentary.

“Then the boys light up” becomes both literal and ironic. The crocodile-versus-shark exchange between Bella and Sister Girl remains unresolved, deliberately unexplained.

---
**SONG 1-05: Evie **(Stevie Wright)

On the highway in Scene 5, Evie claims narrative space. She is not a love interest; she is herself. Jimmy moves from driver to passenger in his own story. The red dirt scrolls into city lights. Bella watches from the property without expression. Davo follows on foot. The gate remains open.

“Evie, let your hair hang down” frames temptation as momentum rather than romance.

---
**SONG 1-06: You Shook Me All Night Long** (AC/DC)

In Scene 6, the city intoxicates. Performed by Evie, the lyric “she was a fast machine” shifts agency toward her. Jimmy loses his shirt—literally—establishing the running gag that will later deepen into emotional weight. Davo, at a parallel table, begins to find himself. The farm briefly appears in the scrolling backdrop and is ignored.

---
**SONG 1-07: Run to Paradise** (Choirboys)

Scene 7 intercuts four simultaneous journeys. Jimmy and Evie chase excitement. Bella rides a bus toward what she cannot yet name. Dad sits alone with unopened bills. Davo opens a different door entirely.

“Run to paradise” becomes ironic: paradise may be a trap. Sister Girl, alone, mutters “A dingo’s got my baby,” playing loneliness as absurdity rather than punchline.

---
**SONG 1-08: Howzat** (Sherbet)

Scene 8 belongs to Bella. She walks through the city without tears, carrying controlled fury. The song is entirely hers. “Now that I found where you’re at… it’s goodbye” lands as clarity, not hysteria. Jimmy realises too late what he has done. Evie’s quiet line—“Yes you did. That’s almost the whole problem, isn’t it”—becomes one of her strongest moments.

---
**SONG 1-09: Dumb Things** (Paul Kelly)

Scene 9 marks loss and reckoning. Dad dies. Jimmy receives the call. The lyric “I lost my shirt” transforms from gag to grief. The humour collapses into consequence. Dad’s echo—“Don’t bore yourself trying to be someone you’re not”—lingers as unfinished guidance.

---
**SONG 1-10: One Word** (Baby Animals)

The bus ride home closes Act One. City lights fade to highway, stars, and red dirt. Jimmy and Davo carry different truths back with them. Davo hints that he found something good in the city. The gate is still open when they arrive.

---
## **ACT TWO**

---
**SONG 2-01: Who Can It Be Now?** (Men at Work)

Scene 1 unfolds on opposite sides of Bella’s door. Jimmy pleads outside. Bella refuses inside. The song is comic and genuinely sad at once. Neither is wrong. “Go ’way, don’t come ’round here no more” becomes boundary rather than rejection. Bella’s silent removal of the kettle when Jimmy mentions steam is small but precise comedy.

---
**SONG 2-02: Do You See What I See? / We Can’t Be Beaten** (Hunters & Collectors / Rose Tattoo)

At the pub, two blokes talk about feelings without quite naming them. Davo comes out in his own idiom. Jimmy admits he loves Bella. “Do you see what I see?” highlights differing perspectives on the same town. “Shoulder to shoulder we’re gonna stand” affirms loyalty. Davo’s “Lock it in, Eddie” keeps the tone buoyant.

---
**SONG 2-03: Solid Rock** (Goanna)

Bella’s centrepiece. Preceded by her declaration, “I’m not his echo. I’m the sound,” the song anchors the show in Country, history, and unbroken line. It is not performed for drama—it is the drama. The didgeridoo intro roots the stage in land. Sister Girl’s “Sixty thousand years. And counting” lands as cultural truth, not commentary.

---
**SONG 2-04: My Girl / Take Me Back** (Hoodoo Gurus / Noiseworks)

At the B&S Ball, Jimmy is all theatrical regret. Bella is present but withholding. The scene is populated by the ghosts of who they were. “My girl don’t love me anymore” exposes loss; “Take me back to you” reveals longing. Bella’s response—“Be better for you. Then maybe I’ll give ya a crack”—defines the terms.

---
**SONG 2-05: Throw Your Arms Around Me / Burn For You** (Hunters & Collectors / INXS)

On a split stage, Bella and Jimmy sing parallel longing without awareness of each other. It is beautiful and unresolved. “We may never meet again” underscores fragility. “Light me and I’ll burn for you” expresses devotion. Their shared image of tilting a hat at the sun briefly unifies them before distance returns.

---
**SONG 2-06: Dreamworld** (Midnight Oil)

Backstage at the B&S, the women choose themselves. The dream of waiting, escaping, and performing for others collapses. “Your dreamworld is just about to end” signals liberation rather than despair. Sister Girl decides to play keys—no permission requested. Her line to Evie, equal parts insult and affection, cements their alliance.

---
 **SONG 2-07: Xanadu** (Olivia Newton-John)

Davo steps into full sequins. The pub erupts in delight, not mockery. That is the point. “A place where nobody dared to go” becomes literal and symbolic. Backstage, Niko urges him to “fall with glitter.” Jimmy says he is proud. Niko slips away quietly after a final joke, his presence complete.

---
**SONG 2-08: Rain / Dancing in the Storm** (Dragon — NZ / Boom Crash Opera)

The storm is the show’s only weather event and its emotional climax. In Rain, Jimmy chooses to stand in the downpour rather than run. “Don’t you go out in the rain” becomes ironic—he stays. His monologue reframes strength as vulnerability. Dancing in the Storm transforms fear into communal celebration as the cast joins him. Bella briefly takes his hand. “Tomorrow, then.” It is enough.

---
**SONG 2-09: Slice of Heaven** (Dave Dobbyn — NZ)

Around a campfire, joy replaces tension. The cast joins in. Niko acknowledges the trans-Tasman joke—half these songs are ours—before disappearing once more. Sister Girl and Niko trade “Sixty thousand years” against “Pavlova since the twenties,” humour resting alongside cultural grounding.

---
**SONG 2-10: You’re the Voice / Two Strong Hearts** (John Farnham)

The finale gathers the full cast and the audience. “You’re the voice” invites participation by design. “Two Strong Hearts” is reframed as connection between people and place rather than romantic pairing. Bella and Jimmy stand side by side, whole and independent. Just before this moment, Jimmy closes the gate. No speech. No explanation. Everything.

---
## **THROUGH-LINE SUMMARY**

The land and home resonate through Sounds of Then, Flame Trees, Solid Rock, Dumb Things, and Two Strong Hearts.

Jimmy’s journey from inherited masculinity to growth runs through Working Class Man, Run to Paradise, Dumb Things, Rain, and Dancing in the Storm.

Unrealised love echoes in Original Sin, Howzat, My Girl, Take Me Back, Throw Your Arms Around Me, and Burn For You.

Female power and independence rise in Howzat, Solid Rock, Dreamworld, and You’re the Voice.

Queer identity moves from subtext in The Boys Light Up to full expression in Xanadu.

Cultural identity and First Nations grounding deepen through Original Sin, Solid Rock, and Dreamworld.

Trans-Tasman comedy and affection thread through Are You Old Enough, Rain, and Slice of Heaven.

The gate, introduced in Scene 1, remains open until it is finally closed just before the finale.
