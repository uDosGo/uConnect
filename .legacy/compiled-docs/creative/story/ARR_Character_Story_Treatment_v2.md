# AUSSIE ROCK RHAPSODY
## Character & Story Treatment (v2.0)

Updated to reflect integrated Book v2.0

---
## CAST SUMMARY

Jimmy is in his early 30s, the conflicted country bloke at the centre of the story. He plays rhythm guitar. He runs away, loses everything, comes home, and closes the gate.

Bella is in her late 20s. She is the heart and conscience of the show. She sings when she chooses to. She never leaves. She waits for Jimmy to deserve her, and eventually allows him the chance to try.

Davo is mid-30s, the best mate who becomes a queer icon. He plays drums. He hides in plain sight, discovers himself in the city, and walks back into town in sequins.

Dad is in his 60s. He represents legacy and becomes a ghostly presence. He plays bass guitar. A stoic Vietnam veteran, he is most powerful in absence. His final words echo throughout the show.

Sister Girl is in her early 20s. She is a cultural truth-speaker who plays keys when she feels like it. Sharp, funny, and rarely wrong, she chooses to play in the finale.

Evie, in her late 20s, is the disruptor and catalyst. She takes lead vocals when she wants them. She arrives like weather and stays because she wants to, not because she has to.

Niko, the Kiwi in his 20s, is the outsider mirror and joy-bringer. He briefly plays acoustic guitar. He appears three times, says exactly what needs to be said, and leaves. He is always right.

The Ensemble represents community, ancestors, ghosts, townsfolk, pub patrons, drag queens, and memory itself. They are the world in which the characters live.

## CHARACTER PROFILES

### JIMMY

Jimmy is in his early 30s, the conflicted country bloke who leads the story. He plays rhythm guitar.

His arc moves from inherited toxic masculinity to emotional vulnerability and, finally, redemption. His journey runs from farm to city, through loss, and back home, where he closes the gate.

He grew up on a drought-stricken farm with a stoic war-veteran father. He learned early that strength meant silence. He left for the city but returned after his father’s death. He is not especially bright, but he is genuine, warm, and capable of growth when someone holds him accountable.

He is torn between staying for the land and fleeing for his own identity. He struggles with guilt and fear of failure. He loves Bella but does not know how to say it, show it, or earn it.

Bella is his first love: unresolved, tender, and challenging. She represents the standard he keeps failing to meet. Davo is his emotional counterweight and knows him better than he knows himself. Dad is the shadow Jimmy has been standing in, the voice he hears when he finally gets quiet. Evie begins as flirtation and becomes clarity; she holds up a mirror he initially resents but ultimately learns from.

Key moments include the fence line conversation with Dad, the quiet grief when he receives the call about his father’s death, the storm monologue in which he realises that strength is admitting fear, and, finally, closing the gate. Throughout the show, a recurring gag about the shirt he lost in the city becomes physical comedy with emotional weight. Davo gives him a new one on the bus home.

In the final beat, Jimmy closes the gate and briefly takes Bella’s hand. He does not fix everything. He decides to be okay. That is the arc.

Jimmy should be played as earnest and warm, never as a fool. The humour comes from sincerity, not stupidity. The audience should love him even at his worst.


---
### BELLA

Bella is in her late 20s, the heart and conscience of the show. She sings when she chooses to.

Her arc moves from silenced patience to empowered truth-teller and cultural anchor. She begins by waiting and watching, learns to say no, chooses herself, and ultimately leaves the door ajar.

She grew up in the same town as Jimmy but from a different world—deeply connected to Country, history, and a strong matriarchal lineage. She is quietly the smartest person in every room and never needs to announce it. She is underestimated but never unaware.

Her central conflict is balancing her love for Jimmy with her self-worth and cultural grounding. She is tired of compromising. She has waited, and that waiting matters, but she will not wait forever.

Jimmy is her unresolved love. She holds space for him without filling it. Sister Girl is her sibling and conscience, sometimes tense but always loyal. Evie begins as friction and becomes trust. The land itself is her constant relationship; she speaks for it.

Her key moments include the dam scene where she anchors the story in sixty thousand years of presence, her silent walk through the city, the Solid Rock monologue where she declares she is not an echo but the sound, the B&S Ball conversation where she demands better, and the finale where she briefly takes Jimmy’s hand. No promises are made; possibility is offered.

Bella ends the show choosing herself, not romantic resolution. She leads the finale with voice and presence. She and Jimmy are two strong hearts, whole in themselves.

She should be played with precision, warmth, and grounded authority. She never explains herself. The comedy lies in how little she says and how deeply it lands. She is the architecture of the story.


---
### DAVO

Davo is in his mid-30s, beginning as comic relief and revealing himself as the emotional surprise. He plays drums.

His arc moves from bogan mate to queer icon and bridge between worlds. He goes from hidden, to hinting, to city discovery, to walking out in sequins fully seen.

In town, he is the easygoing tradie with a big laugh. Secretly, he has a drag persona only the sheep have witnessed. The city finally gives him a mirror that reflects who he truly is.

His conflict is fear of rejection versus the need to be seen. He hides behind humour, loving his mate, town, and life while living only half of it.

Jimmy is the reaction that matters most to him. Niko becomes a mischievous sidekick and emotional anchor, encouraging him to fall—with glitter. Evie recognises him from the start; they are rock and roll soulmates.

His key scenes include the milkbar shrug of vulnerability, the unnoticed city bar conversation, the backstage confession that he only ever danced with sheep, the sequinned reveal that brings the pub to its feet, and the finale where he stands unapologetically front and centre.

In the final beat, he is fully present, fully himself. The crowd loves him. Jimmy is proud. Niko nods. That is everything he needed.

He must be played as physical, loud, and warm. The comedy is in his size and sincerity; the emotional punch comes from how long he has been hiding.


---
### DAD

Dad is in his 60s, a ghostly presence and legacy figure. He plays bass guitar.

He does not change. He represents the generational weight Jimmy must face and ultimately honour. He moves from presence to absence to echo and release.

A hardened Vietnam veteran turned farmer, he is emotionally shut down. He rarely says “I love you,” but carries it in calloused hands. The land is his language. His puns are defence mechanisms he does not recognise as puns.

His famous last words are “Shut the bloody gate.” The gate remains open for the entire show until Jimmy closes it just before the finale.

Dad embodies the pressure of masculinity, silence, and duty. He is the model Jimmy tries to follow without understanding the cost.

His moments are spare but heavy: with machinery, at the fence line, in echoes that urge Jimmy not to bore himself trying to be someone he is not, and finally in the bass line that carries memory and release.

He should be played with minimal movement and maximum weight. His bass guitar is his voice.


---
### SISTER GIRL

Sister Girl is in her early 20s, Bella’s sibling and a cultural truth-speaker. She plays keys when she chooses, ultimately deciding to play in the finale.

Her arc is one of visibility. She begins as observer and commentator, becomes truth-speaker, and ends as player.

Growing up in Bella’s shadow, she developed her own fire. She speaks truth with sharp humour and zero patience for nonsense. She represents the now-generation.

Her conflict is wanting to be seen as herself, not only as Bella’s sister. She claims her moment by choosing to play keys in the finale without asking permission.

Her recurring line, “Sixty thousand years,” is cultural anchoring, never a joke. Other lines balance absurd humour and pointed truth. In the finale, she simply sits at the keys and plays. No announcement.

She must be fast, sharp, and grounded. The audience should trust her immediately.


---
### EVIE

Evie is in her late 20s, the disruptor and truth mirror. She takes lead vocals when she wants them.

Her arc moves from escapist to realist to self-accepting powerhouse. She arrives, seduces, gains clarity, builds friendship, and stays on her own terms.

She comes from the city with glamour as armour, witty and loud, brilliant at hiding pain. Initially a wildcard, she becomes glue.

Her conflict lies between deflecting intimacy and wanting to belong. She has been the punchline long enough to forget she can choose otherwise.

Bella becomes her ally. Jimmy becomes a mirror for reckoning rather than romance. Davo is her rock and roll equal. Sister Girl meets her insult for insult, respect for respect.

In the final beat, she chooses friendship over romance. She remains because she wants to. In the finale she stands front of stage, eyes closed, free.

She should be played as direct and unpredictable. She does not need fixing; she needs space.


---
### NIKO

Niko, the Kiwi in his 20s, is the outsider mirror and joy-bringer. He briefly plays acoustic guitar.

His arc is simple: passing through, saying the necessary thing, and leaving.

A New Zealander with a big heart and bigger laugh, he speaks of ancestors as if they are close at hand. Seen as comic, he carries deep wisdom and mana.

He appears at the dam, backstage encouraging Davo, at the campfire naming the New Zealand songs and addressing the audience, and finally in the wings during the finale. His campfire scene with Sister Girl mirrors the show’s deepest themes of land, memory, and belonging.

He resolves nothing. He names what others cannot. He leaves at exactly the right time.

He must be played with joy and gravitas; his depth should catch the audience off guard.


---
## ENSEMBLE

The ensemble represents community, chorus, ancestors, memory, townsfolk, pub patrons, drag queens, and ghosts. Doubling is appropriate for minor roles. Movement should visually support the principal arcs. They appear as ancestors during Solid Rock, as the erupting crowd in Xanadu, as figures joining Jimmy in the storm, and as the community that was always present in the finale.

## STORY TREATMENT SUMMARY

Act One is the leaving. The world is introduced, Bella speaks first, and the band establishes place. Jimmy and Dad fail to articulate what they feel, and the gate opens. At the dam, tension between Jimmy and Bella is immense, grounded in deep time. Evie arrives at the milkbar and the room shifts. Jimmy leaves for the city; Davo walks. In the city, Jimmy loses his shirt and ignores the farm looming behind him. Stories spiral in parallel. Bella visits and leaves without drama. Dad dies. Jimmy returns home on the bus. The gate remains open.

Act Two is the return. Jimmy knocks; Bella hides. They sing at the same door. At the pub, men talk about feelings. Davo admits who he is. Bella stands on Country and declares her voice. At the B&S Ball, she demands better. Songs cross without resolution. Evie and Bella find solidarity. Davo walks out in sequins. Jimmy stands in the storm and chooses not to run. Niko names the New Zealand songs and disappears. Jimmy closes the gate. Bella arrives. Tomorrow is possible. The finale gathers everyone still here. The encore breaks the fourth wall with rapid puns and celebration.


---
## THEMATIC ARCHITECTURE

The women are the architecture. They do not announce their competence; they embody it. Bella’s arrival in the city shifts the story. Evie’s return pivots Act Two. Sister Girl decides when doors open. The men are loved and funny, but the women hold the structure.

The gate is a physical metaphor. Dad’s instruction to shut it is ignored until the final moments. Its closing is not explained or commented on. It simply happens.

Sixty thousand years is cultural anchoring, not a punchline. It lands differently each time.

The puns operate as defence mechanisms and accidents. They are never explained. They either land or they do not.

Niko always leaves. He appears, speaks truth, and goes. His exit is right, not tragic.


---
## ENDING PHILOSOPHY

No one ends up with a partner. Jimmy and Bella hold hands briefly. The moment is real and suggests possibility without promising it. The audience may want them together. That wanting is the point. Life does not always deliver the expected ending. Sometimes it offers something better: the understanding that you were whole all along.
